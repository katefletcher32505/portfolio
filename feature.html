It Was Never Just Hunger

A longform feature exploring Prader-Willi Syndrome, caregiving, and the lived reality behind medical language.

About the Piece

This feature article translates medical complexity into human experience. Centered on caregiving and Prader-Willi Syndrome, the piece explores how clinical language often fails to capture the emotional, psychological, and lived realities of the condition.

Rather than presenting the condition abstractly, the article grounds its analysis in narrative—examining hunger, behavior, family dynamics, and the long-term impact of care.

Why This Matters

I wrote this piece before my brother, Shields, died from complications of Prader-Willi Syndrome in October 2025. At the time, I was trying to make sense of a condition that is often explained clinically, but rarely understood in terms of lived experience—what it means for families, for caregivers, and for the people living inside it every day.

After his death, the meaning of this work shifted. What began as an effort to translate medical complexity into human language has become something more personal: a way of preserving experience, of naming what often goes unspoken, and of refusing to let those realities be flattened into diagnosis alone.

This piece reflects my broader commitment to making complex systems—especially medical and psychological ones—understandable, human, and ethically grounded. Moving forward, I hope to continue this work in ways that support others who have lived alongside Prader-Willi Syndrome, and to create space for experiences that are often overlooked or simplified in public and clinical narratives.
